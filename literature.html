The Literature of Tolkien

Middle-Earth Fiction

The Hobbit: or There and Back Again. George Allen and Unwin, London, 1937. (Revised in 1951 and 1966, and reprinted frequently.) A beloved children’s story that originated from a blank exam page, recounting Bilbo Baggins’ quest with dwarves to reclaim the Lonely Mountain from the dragon Smaug.

The Fellowship of the Ring: First Part of The Lord of the Rings. George Allen and Unwin, London, 1954. (Second edition, 1966.) A world-renowned book continuing the story of the ring discovered in The Hobbit and detailing the journey of Frodo Baggins and his companions.

The Two Towers: Second Part of The Lord of the Rings. George Allen and Unwin, London, 1954. (Second edition, 1966.) The ongoing saga from The Fellowship of the Ring, following Frodo and his companions on their continued adventures.

The Return of the King: Third Part of The Lord of the Rings. George Allen and Unwin, London, 1955. (Second edition, 1966.) The final installment of the epic saga, concluding the journey that began in The Fellowship of the Ring.

The Silmarillion. Edited by Christopher Tolkien. George Allen and Unwin, London, 1977. A compilation of Tolkien’s mythological tales detailing the beginnings of Middle-earth and the First Ages, compiled by his son.

The Book of Lost Tales, Part I. Edited by Christopher Tolkien. The History of Middle-earth: Vol. 1. George Allen and Unwin, London, 1983.

The Book of Lost Tales, Part II. Edited by Christopher Tolkien. The History of Middle-earth: Vol. 2. George Allen and Unwin, London, 1984.

The Lays of Beleriand. Edited by Christopher Tolkien. The History of Middle-earth: Vol. 3. George Allen and Unwin, London, 1985.

The Shaping of Middle-earth. Edited by Christopher Tolkien. The History of Middle-earth: Vol. 4. George Allen and Unwin, London, 1986.

The Lost Road and Other Writings. Edited by Christopher Tolkien. The History of Middle-earth: Vol. 5. Unwin Hyman, London, 1987.

The Return of the Shadow. Edited by Christopher Tolkien. The History of Middle-earth: Vol. 6. Unwin Hyman, London, 1988.

The Treason of Isengard. Edited by Christopher Tolkien. The History of Middle-earth: Vol. 7. Unwin Hyman, London, 1989.

The War of the Ring. Edited by Christopher Tolkien. The History of Middle-earth: Vol. 8. Unwin Hyman, London, 1990.

Sauron Defeated. Edited by Christopher Tolkien. The History of Middle-earth: Vol. 9. HarperCollins, London, 1992.

Morgoth’s Ring. Edited by Christopher Tolkien. The History of Middle-earth: Vol. 10. HarperCollins, London, 1993.

The War of the Jewels. Edited by Christopher Tolkien. The History of Middle-earth: Vol. 11. HarperCollins, London, 1994.

The Peoples of Middle-earth. Edited by Christopher Tolkien. The History of Middle-earth: Vol. 12. HarperCollins, London, 1996.

The Children of Húrin. Edited by Christopher Tolkien, with illustrations by Alan Lee. HarperCollins, London, 2007. A seamless novel of the story of Túrin Turambar, compiled from various versions by Christopher Tolkien.

Beren and Lúthien. Edited by Christopher Tolkien. HarperCollins, London, 2017. This volume meticulously restores Tolkien’s manuscripts into a continuous narrative of the Beren and Lúthien tale. Despite the publisher’s description as a standalone story, some readers expected a more cohesive presentation akin to The Children of Húrin, rather than a compilation of variant versions arranged in historical sequence.

The Fall of Gondolin. Edited by Christopher Tolkien. HarperCollins, London, 2018. Similar to Beren and Lúthien, this book presents a sequence of variant versions of the tale of Gondolin, assembled to provide a historical view of Tolkien’s evolving narrative.

The Nature of Middle-earth. Edited by Carl Hostetter. HarperCollins, London, 2021. This collection represents Tolkien’s final writings on Middle-earth, exploring a broad range of topics about the world and its inhabitants. Although organized, it is best approached as a book to dip into rather than read cover-to-cover.

The Fall of Númenor. Edited by Brian Sibley. HarperCollins, London, 2022. This volume consolidates all published texts related to the Second Age of Middle-earth, accompanied by a unifying commentary.

Short Stories and Other Literature

Farmer Giles of Ham. Allen and Unwin, London, 1949. A whimsical medieval tale about a farmer's adventures involving giants, dragons, and courtly intrigue.

Tree and Leaf. Allen and Unwin, London, 1964. (New edition including “Mythopoeia” by Unwin Hyman, London, 1988.) Reprints Tolkien’s lecture “On Fairy-Stories” and his short story “Leaf by Niggle."

Smith of Wootton Major. Allen and Unwin, London, 1967. A short story about a small English village, its smith, and his adventures in Faery.

Leaf by Niggle. , London, 2016. This is the first stand-alone edition of Tolkien’s short story, released to coincide with a touring stage production of the story. It includes an afterword by Tom Shippey, which was originally part of the 2008 edition of Tales from the Perilous Realm.

Roverandom. by Christina Scull and Wayne Hammond. HarperCollins, London, 1998. A story about a toy dog’s adventures, written to cheer up Tolkien’s son after losing a toy on vacation.

Poetry

J.R.R. Tolkien's poetry is an integral part of his fictional worlds, particularly in The Lord of the Rings, The Hobbit, and The Silmarillion. His poems often reflect the mythic and legendary themes of his works, focusing on the beauty of nature, heroism, and melancholy. They also draw from various linguistic traditions, with many being written in Elvish, Old English, and other languages he invented.

Themes in Tolkien's Poetry:

Myth and Legend: Tolkien's poetry is often set in the mythical past of Middle-earth, invoking ancient heroes, battles, and the rise and fall of civilizations.

Nature and the Passing of Time: Many poems express Tolkien's love of nature, with vivid descriptions of forests, rivers, and stars, as well as a recurring focus on the bittersweet passage of time.

Journeys and Adventure: As with his prose, much of his poetry revolves around quests, journeys, and exploration, capturing the essence of adventure.

Lament and Loss: A melancholy tone pervades much of his poetry, reflecting the decline of old ways, the fading of magic, and the loss of loved ones.

Song-like Structure: Many of Tolkien’s poems are meant to be songs, which is why music and verse often play an important role in his stories.

Notable Poems by Tolkien:

"The Road Goes Ever On": A poem from The Lord of the Rings, this piece represents the theme of endless journeys and adventure. It is sung by both Bilbo and Frodo Baggins.

"The Road goes ever on and on,
Down from the door where it began.
Now far ahead the Road has gone,
And I must follow, if I can…"

"Song of Durin": This poem from The Lord of the Rings celebrates the ancient Dwarf-lord Durin and his people. It is a nostalgic reflection on the lost greatness of the Dwarves.

"The world was fair, the mountains tall,
In Elder Days before the fall
Of mighty kings in Nargothrond
And Gondolin, who now beyond
The Western Seas have passed away..."

"Lament for Boromir": This mournful poem is sung by Aragorn, Legolas, and Gimli in honor of the fallen hero Boromir.

"Through Rohan over fen and field where the long grass grows,
The West Wind comes walking, and about the walls it goes."

"A Elbereth Gilthoniel": A hymn to Varda (Elbereth), queen of the stars, this Elvish poem is a prayer that captures the spiritual and otherworldly aspect of the Elves.

"A Elbereth Gilthoniel,
silivren penna míriel
o menel aglar elenath!..."

"The Lay of Leithian": A long narrative poem that tells the tragic love story of Beren and Lúthien, two central characters in The Silmarillion. This epic poem captures love, loss, and the courage of two individuals against insurmountable odds.

Style and Influence

Tolkien’s poetry was influenced by ancient epics such as Beowulf, medieval ballads, and Norse mythology. His use of alliteration, meter, and rhyme often mirrors these older forms, grounding his invented world in a deep sense of history and tradition. In addition to the poems embedded within his stories, Tolkien also published a volume of his non-Middle-earth poetry titled The Adventures of Tom Bombadil (1962), which collects lighthearted verse about various creatures and events in his world.

Academic Writing and Constructed Languages

A Middle English Vocabulary. The Clarendon Press, Oxford, 1922. Bound with Fourteenth Century Verse & Prose, edited by Kenneth Sisam (Oxford University Press). A glossary for students of Middle English words.

Finn and Hengest: The Fragment and the Episode. Edited by Alan Bliss. George Allen and Unwin, London, 1982. Tolkien’s translations and commentaries on Old English texts from his 1920s lectures.

The Legend of Sigurd and Gudrún. Edited by Christopher Tolkien. HarperCollins, London, 2009. Tolkien’s retelling of the ancient northern legends of Sigurd and Gudrún.

The Fall of Arthur. Edited by Christopher Tolkien. HarperCollins, London, 2013. A compilation of Tolkien’s versions of the Arthurian cycle’s end, featuring commentary and essays by Christopher Tolkien.

Beowulf: A Translation and Commentary, together with Sellic Spell. Edited by Christopher Tolkien. HarperCollins, London, 2014. Includes Tolkien’s 1926 translation of Beowulf, with commentary and notes, and his imaginative ‘Sellic Spell.’

Tolkien On Fairy-Stories. Edited by Verlyn Flieger and Douglas A. Anderson. HarperCollins, London, 2014.

The Story of Kullervo. Edited by Verlyn Flieger. HarperCollins, London, 2015. The first publication of Tolkien’s fantasy story based on the Finnish Kalevala, which inspired elements of The Children of Húrin and Roverandom.

The Battle of Maldon, together with The Homecoming of Beorhtnoth. Edited by Peter Grybauskas. HarperCollins, London, 2023. This book features the first publication of Tolkien’s prose version of the 10th-century alliterative poem The Battle of Maldon, along with his sequel, The Homecoming of Beorhtnoth.

A Secret Vice: Tolkien on Invented Languages. Edited by Dimitra Fimi and Andrew Higgins. HarperCollins, London, 2016. This comprehensive edition expands on Tolkien’s 1931 lecture "A Hobby for the Home," which was previously published as "A Secret Vice" in The Monsters and the Critics and Other Essays. This new edition includes previously unpublished notes and drafts, such as the "Essay on Phonetic Symbolism," and provides a critical analysis of Tolkien’s views on invented languages and their central role in his creative process, along with details on the lecture’s historical context.

Languages of Tolkien's middle-EarthA reference guide on the fourteen languages created by Tolkien. A study by Ruth S. Noel, 1974.

Art

J.R.R. Tolkien was not only a renowned author but also a talented artist. His artwork is often linked to his famous literary works, particularly The Hobbit and The Lord of the Rings series. Tolkien’s illustrations capture vivid landscapes, detailed maps, and his imagined world of Middle-earth, reflecting his deep love for nature and mythology.

Key Characteristics of Tolkien’s Artwork:

Landscapes and Nature: Many of his illustrations depict forests, mountains, rivers, and mythical locations, emphasizing natural beauty and mystical environments.

Maps: Tolkien created highly detailed maps of Middle-earth, which serve as a visual aid for readers to follow the journeys of his characters. These maps are iconic and intricately designed.

Architectural Elements: His work often includes ancient, detailed structures like towers, castles, and cities, adding to the world-building aspect of his stories.

Watercolor and Ink: Tolkien’s art typically used watercolors or ink, giving his pieces a delicate and ethereal quality.

Symbolism: His art, like his writing, often included symbolic and mythological elements, like runes, stars, and intricate patterns.

Tolkien’s artwork, while not as well-known as his writing, offers a glimpse into the visual imagination behind his legendary stories. Some of his most famous works include illustrations from The Hobbit, such as the cover featuring Smaug and the Lonely Mountain. His artistic style has a gentle, almost whimsical quality, yet with a sense of grandeur fitting for his epic tales.
